Rational

Background

Rational functions are those who can be defined by a rational fraction. A rational fraction is represented by one polynomial divided by another. A rational function has the following base form. y = 1⁄x . In transformation form it has the form y = a⁄b(x-c) + d.


The main use of rational functions is to solve problems involving time and rates, they allow us to figure out how long it will take an individual to complete a task. A logistic function also has a similar form to rational function, and they have many uses. Logistic functions can model population growth, be used in artificial intelligence, and in physics to make statistics on a system in thermal equilibrium.


How to Graph

Every rational function has at least one vertical asymptote, as well as a horizontal asymptote.

The vertical asymptote(s) are determined by the denominator of the rational function. At any point when the denominator is 0, a vertical asymptote is created. For example, if the equation is 1⁄(x-2) , then it would have a vertical asymptote at x = 2 because the denominator cannot be equal to 0 at any time.

To find the horizontal asymptote, you must determine whether the largest power in the numerator and the largest power in the denominator.

  • If the power of the numerator is larger, then x = 0 is the horizontal asymptote.
  • If the numerator and denominator have the same largest exponent, then take the coefficient of the variable with the largest exponent from the numerator and divide it with the coefficient of the variable with the largest exponent from the denominator.
  • If the power of the denominator is larger, then there is no horizontal asymptote.
The simplest rational function, 1⁄x , has points at (-1, -1), (-.5, -2), (.5, 2), and (1, 1). Utilising these key points is essential when shifting or transforming a rational function.

In the form y = a⁄b(x-c) + d...
  • a represents the vertical stretch (multiply y by a)
  • b represents the horizontal stretch (divide x by b)
  • c represents the horizontal shift
  • d represents the vertical shift


How to Solve

To solve a rational function, you must first make sure that the denominator doesn't equal 0, or else you won't be able to solve it. When solving for x, first add the d to y. Then remove the denominator by multiplying both sides of the equation by the denominator. After that it's a matter of simple math and intuition.

Example 1:
2 = 3⁄2(x - 5) - 6
Add 6 to 2
8 = 3⁄2(x - 5)
Multiply 8 by 2(x - 5)
16(x - 5) = 3
Divide by 16
3⁄16 = x - 5
Add 5
x = 83⁄16


Example 2:
1 = 3⁄6(x - 2) + 5
Add 5 to 1
6 = 3⁄6(x - 2)
Multiply 6 by 6(x - 2)
36(x - 2) = 3
Divide by 36
3⁄36 = x - 2
Add 2
x = 25⁄12


Example 3:
3 = (x - 1)⁄(x - 3)
Multiply 3 by (x - 3)
3(x - 3) = (x - 1)
Distribute
3x - 9 = x - 1 Add 9 to -1 and subtract x from 3x.
2x = 8
Divide by 2
x = 4


Transformations

Take into consideration the following rational function:
 y = 2⁄3(x+4) - 4

We see that this function has gone through a:

  • Vertical stretch by 2 (multiply all y values by 2)
  • Horizontal compression by 3 (multiply all x values by 3)
  • Horizontal shift left 4
  • Vertical shift down 4
When graphing, shifts must be done after stretches and compressions.
In this function, the new horizontal asymptote is y = -4
